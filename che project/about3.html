Pseudocereals And Grains

Several plant foods have been extensively studied and introduced into the human diet as unconventional protein sources due to their excellent nutritional composition. Among these foods, sorghum and the so called pseudocereals amaranth, quinoa, and chia have amino acid compositions with high bioavailability and content of essential amino acids (Belton and Taylor, 2004, Castro‐Alba et al., 2019Castro‐Alba et al., 2019Castro‐Alba et al., 2019, López et al., 2018, Martínez-Villaluenga et al., 2020), which favors their use as animal protein substitutes. These pseudocereals and grain stand out among others because of their high amount of sulfur amino acids and minerals, such as calcium, iron, and zinc, in addition to vitamins and phytochemicals (Castro‐Alba et al., 2019Castro‐Alba et al., 2019Castro‐Alba et al., 2019, Girard and Awika, 2018). Additionally, they can contribute to improving the consumption of fibers, polyphenols, minerals, and vitamins. Amaranth, chia, and quinoa have grown in tropical and subtropical regions and have been included in the Incas’ diet for centuries (López et al., 2018).

The Maya were probably the first people to use amaranth as a productive crop, and the Incas and the Aztecs later adopted it (Escudero, de Arellano, Luco, Giménez, & Mucciarelli, 2004, Martinez-Lopez et al., 2020). Currently, these foods are grown not only in Mexico and Central America, but also in Bolivia, Argentina, Ecuador, Guatemala, and Peru (Morales et al., 2020, Orona-Tamayo et al., 2019, Tang and Tsao, 2017). Amaranth and quinoa have been used recently due to their potential health benefits, such as lowering cholesterol, anticancer, antioxidant, and antihypertensive properties (Cazarin et al., 2012, Morales et al., 2020). In 2013, the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization encouraged the planting and research of amaranth, quinoa, and their products to be adapted in most tropical regions and temperate climates in the world (Tang & Tsao, 2017). These products have gained prominence in the consumer market because they are gluten-free, which makes them an alternative source in the diet of celiac patients, contributing to an adequate nutrient intake (Filho et al., 2017, López et al., 2019, López et al., 2018, Niro et al., 2019, Tang and Tsao, 2017). The expected annual growth rate for the “gluten-free” market is 9.1% from 2019 to 2025 (Martínez-Villaluenga et al., 2020). These pseudocereals can be consumed mainly as flour or grains/seeds, used in cereal-based preparations and products, such as soups, yogurts, salads, cakes, and bread (Niro et al., 2019, Pereira et al., 2019), increasing the variety of dishes and preparations for celiac people. Amaranth is a dicotyledonous plant from the Amaranthaceae family that comprises more than 60 species of the genus Amaranthus worldwide. Among the Amaranthus species used for human consumption are Amaranthus caudatus, Amaranthus cruentus, and Amaranthus hypochondriacus (Orona-Tamayo et al., 2019). They are composed of 13.2 to 18.4% of proteins relatively rich in lysine (limiting amino acid in cereals), tryptophan, and sulfur amino acids (Orona-Tamayo et al., 2019, Quiroga et al., 2017, Soares et al., 2015, Tang and Tsao, 2017); 2.2 to 6.7% of fibers; up to 13% of lipids rich in linoleic acid; and 57 to 65.3% of carbohydrates mainly constituted by starch (Orona-Tamayo et al., 2019, Tang and Tsao, 2017). Minerals represent approximately 1.5% of the amaranth composition, especially calcium, iron, manganese, magnesium, potassium, and sodium (Orona-Tamayo et al., 2019, Tang and Tsao, 2017). In fact, compared to other grains, amaranth has the highest amount of gluten-free protein, calcium, dietary fibers, and essential amino acids necessary for healthy living (Jimoh, Afolayan, & Lewu, 2018). The leaves of Amaranthus deflexus L. can also be eaten as stews or steamed (Jimoh et al., 2018). Quinoa belongs to the Chenopodiaceae family, genus Chenopodium quinoa wild, and it is a tetrapod species that has been extensively explored due to its easy adaptation and growth in different environmental conditions (Dakhili et al., 2019, Pereira et al., 2019). Its excellent nutritional composition is based on essential amino acids, such as lysine, tryptophan, and cysteine, with an average proximate composition of 14.1% proteins, 6.2% lipids, 64.2% carbohydrates, 7% fibers, and 1.3% minerals (Tang & Tsao, 2017), such as calcium, magnesium, iron, manganese, copper, and potassium (Dakhili et al., 2019, Filho et al., 2017). In addition to the composition in macronutrients and micronutrients, amaranth and quinoa have bioactive compounds such as phenolic acids, flavonoids, anthocyanins, tannins, and phytosterols (Tang & Tsao, 2017). These bioactive compounds possess a potent antioxidant capacity and can reduce the risk of developing degenerative diseases associated with oxidative stress (Filho et al., 2017). Flavonoids, such as quercetin and kaempferol, stand out for their antioxidant capacity in quinoa, while in amaranth, this activity is mainly exerted by caffeic, p-hydroxybenzoic, and ferulic acids (Filho et al., 2017). The bioactive peptides with antihypertensive, antimicrobial, hypocholesterolemic, antioxidant, hypoglycemic, and immunomodulatory properties are also noteworthy (Orona-Tamayo et al., 2019, Quiroga et al., 2017, Soares et al., 2015). In quinoa, low molecular weight peptides (5 to 9 amino acid residues) released by alcalase action have the antioxidant capacity and angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitor activity (Orona-Tamayo et al., 2019). Recent studies observed a potent in vitro hypoglycemic effect through the inhibition of key enzymes such as dipeptidyl peptidase IV (DPP-IV), α-amylase, and α-glucosidase after gastroduodenal protein digestion (López et al., 2019, Morales et al., 2020). This activity was also reported by the inhibition of α-amylase in amaranth grains (Martínez-Villaluenga et al., 2020). The antioxidant capacity of peptides rich in histidine from the 11S globulin fraction was reported by Silva-Sánchez et al. (2008), while other authors found the same capacity for all protein fractions of amaranth (Orona-Tamayo et al., 2019). Tri- and tetrapeptides showed in vitro hypocholesterolemic capacity by inhibiting 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl coenzyme A reductase (HMG-CoA reductase), which is responsible for endogenous cholesterol synthesis (Morales et al., 2020, Soares et al., 2015). On the other hand, in vivo studies suggest a hypocholesterolemic effect through the action of fibers, inhibiting the ileal reabsorption of bile acids and favoring the control of cholesterol synthesis and catabolism (Cazarin et al., 2012, Sisti et al., 2019). Chia (Salvia hispanica L.) is an herbaceous plant from the Lamiaceae family, native to Mexico, Guatemala, and California (Koner, Dash, Priya, & Rajeswari, 2019). Its grains contain several bioactive compounds such as quercetin, kaempferol, myricetin, chlorogenic, caffeoylquinic, salvianolic, and protocatechuic acids, in addition to fibers and polyunsaturated fatty acids, mainly linolenic and oleic acids (Marineli et al., 2014, Miller et al., 2017, Oliveira-Alves et al., 2017). Chia showed positive effects against induced obesity in animal models (Carnier et al., 2017), and some of the outcomes were improvement of acute dyslipidemia and steatohepatitis in rats (Fernández‐Martínez et al., 2019), reduction adipose tissue inflammation (Martino et al., 2020), and improvement of white adipose tissue browning in high-fat diet-induced-obese mice (de Souza et al., 2020). Recent clinical studies have shown that the administration of chia seed could reduce the risk factors to develop non-communicable diseases (Lombardo, Creus, Oliva, & Chicco, 2019). Chia intake can manage obesity and diabetes, promoting weight loss and reducing risk factors such as glycemia (Vuksan et al., 2017). This ancient grain has also been used beyond health purposes and has several technological applications in food products as films (Urbizo-Reyes, San Martin-González, Garcia-Bravo, & Liceaga, 2020), meat products (Barros et al., 2018, Fernández-López et al., 2019), dairy products (Cardoso et al., 2020), gluten free formulations (Menga et al., 2017), and even as nanoparticles for antibacterial applications (Hernández-Morales et al., 2019). Sorghum is considered suitable for sustainable production systems and can be a source of bioactive compounds associated with the prevention of chronic diseases (Girard & Awika, 2018). It contains resistant starch, soluble and insoluble dietary fiber (with beta-glucans), phenolic compounds such as luteolinidine, apigeninidine, 5-methoxylutelinidine, 7-methoxyapigeninidine, luteoline, apigenin, naringenin, and eriodictyol, which are responsible for metabolism modulation and health improvement (Przybylska-Balcerek et al., 2019, Teixeira Nde et al., 2016, Wu et al., 2016). Humans have been using sorghum for a long time as food, but the consumption is lower than that of other grains, such as wheat. Nevertheless, its use as human food is rising, mainly due to the replacement of gluten-free wheat (McGinnis & Painter, 2020). Additionally, it can be used in food products including fermented products (Adebo, 2020), cooked products (Weerasooriya, Bean, Nugusu, Ioerger, & Tesso, 2018), food complements (Okoth, Ochola, Gikonyo, & Makokha, 2017), and pasta (Palavecino, Ribotta, León, & Bustos, 2019). In general, there are reports about many health-promoting benefits of sorghum ingestion, such as antioxidant and anti-inflammatory capacities, cancer, dyslipidemia, and cardiovascular disease prevention, including actions against diabetes and obesity (de Sousa et al., 2018, Smolensky et al., 2018, Xiong et al., 2019). Concerning obesity and insulin resistance, the known health benefits reported for this grain include improving antioxidant response and reducing glucose digestion (Moraes et al., 2015). Furthermore, sorghum has effectively reduced liver fat, plasma lipid peroxidation and fasting glucose, improved glucose tolerance, insulin resistance, and islet insulin secretion in an obesity animal model induced by a high-fat diet (Moraes et al., 2018).
